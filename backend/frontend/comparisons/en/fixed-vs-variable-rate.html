Frequently Asked Questions

What is the main difference between fixed and variable rate mortgages?
The primary difference is stability versus flexibility. Fixed rate mortgages lock in a single interest rate for the entire loan term, guaranteeing identical monthly payments throughout. Variable rate mortgages feature an initial rate that adjusts periodically (usually annually or semi-annually) based on market indices, causing payments to fluctuate over time. Fixed rates provide predictability, while variable rates offer potential savings if rates decline.
Can I switch from a variable rate to a fixed rate mortgage?
Yes, many borrowers convert from variable to fixed rate mortgages through a process called conversion or refinancing. Some lenders offer streamlined conversion options for existing customers without requiring a full application. However, if you refinance through a new lender, you'll typically incur closing costs and a new credit check. The best time to convert is when you anticipate rising rates or want to lock in favorable fixed rates before they increase.
How much can my payment increase with a variable rate mortgage?
Payment increases depend on the specific mortgage terms and how much rates rise. Most variable mortgages include rate caps that limit how much the rate can increase per adjustment period and over the loan's lifetime. For example, a mortgage might cap increases at 0.5% per adjustment and 2% over the loan's life. However, without caps, payments could increase substantially if interest rates spike significantly. Always review your mortgage documents to understand applicable caps and adjustment periods.
Should I choose a fixed or variable rate if I'm unsure about future rates?
If you're uncertain about future rate movements and want to minimize stress, a fixed rate mortgage is generally the safer choice. Fixed rates provide certainty and protection regardless of market conditions, making them ideal for risk-averse borrowers. However, if you have financial flexibility, strong cash flow, and can comfortably absorb potential payment increases, a variable rate might offer better long-term value. Consider your personal risk tolerance and financial situation rather than trying to predict rate movements, which even experts frequently miscalculate.
Are there any hidden costs I should know about with either mortgage type?
Both fixed and variable mortgages may include various costs beyond the interest rate, such as origination fees, appraisal costs, title insurance, and closing costs. Variable mortgages might also charge adjustment fees when rates change. Additionally, if you need to break a fixed-rate mortgage early, you could face significant prepayment penalties, sometimes exceeding thousands of dollars. With variable mortgages, unexpected payment increases could strain budgets if not properly planned. Always review the full Loan Estimate and disclosure documents before committing to any mortgage.
