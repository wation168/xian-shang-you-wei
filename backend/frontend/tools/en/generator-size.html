Understanding Generator Sizing

Selecting the right generator size is crucial for ensuring reliable power supply to your home or business. A generator that is too small will struggle to power your devices and may shut down unexpectedly, while an oversized generator wastes fuel and money. Our calculator helps you find the optimal balance by considering both your continuous running watts and peak starting watts.

Running Watts vs. Starting Watts

Running watts represent the continuous power consumption of your appliances during normal operation. Starting watts, also called inrush or peak load, are the additional watts required when motors and compressors first start up. For example, an air conditioner might require 3,500 watts to run continuously but needs 8,500 watts at startup. Your generator must handle both these requirements to function properly.

The 20% Buffer Recommendation

We recommend adding a 20% buffer to your calculated minimum generator size. This buffer serves multiple purposes: it prevents the generator from running at maximum capacity continuously, which extends its lifespan; it provides headroom for unexpected power surges; and it allows for future expansion of your power needs. Running a generator at 80% of its rated capacity is industry best practice for optimal efficiency and durability.

How to Use This Calculator

Start by listing all the devices and appliances you plan to run from your generator. Look up the wattage requirements for each device—this information is typically found on the device itself or in the manual. Add up all the running wattages to get your total running watts. For starting watts, identify devices with motors or compressors and add their peak wattage requirements. Enter both figures into our calculator to instantly determine your ideal generator size.

Common Wattage Requirements

Understanding typical power consumption helps you calculate accurately. A standard refrigerator uses about 600 watts running, a microwave uses 1,000-2,000 watts, an electric water heater uses 3,500-5,500 watts, and a central air conditioner uses 3,500-5,500 watts running with 8,000-15,000 watts peak. Window units typically need 1,200-1,500 watts running. Small power tools vary from 500-1,500 watts, while larger tools like table saws can reach 2,000-4,000 watts. Always check manufacturer specifications for precise figures.

Choosing the Right Generator Type

Once you know your required capacity, you'll need to choose between portable and standby generators. Portable generators offer flexibility and lower cost but require manual setup and fuel. Standby generators provide automatic backup power and can run on natural gas or propane, making them convenient for whole-home use. Consider your primary needs: occasional backup power might suit a portable model, while continuous reliability typically requires a standby unit. Inverter generators are excellent for sensitive electronics and RVs due to their stable power output.

Installation and Safety Considerations

Proper installation is essential for safe generator operation. Always place generators outside, at least 20 feet away from windows, doors, and vents to prevent carbon monoxide accumulation. Never refuel while the generator is running or hot. Use heavy-duty extension cords rated for outdoor use if needed. Install a transfer switch for standby generators to prevent backfeeding power into the utility lines. Consult a licensed electrician for permanent installations. Regular maintenance including oil changes, fuel stabilizer addition, and periodic testing ensures your generator performs when needed.

FAQ

What's the difference between running watts and starting watts?
Running watts are the continuous power needed to operate an appliance normally, while starting watts (also called inrush watts) are the additional power required when motors or compressors first start. Starting watts are typically much higher and last only a few seconds. Your generator must handle both to operate effectively.
Why do you recommend a 20% buffer?
A 20% buffer prevents your generator from running at maximum capacity continuously, which extends equipment lifespan and improves efficiency. It also provides headroom for unexpected power surges and allows for future expansion of your power needs without requiring a new generator.
What happens if I buy a generator that's too small?
An undersized generator will struggle to power all your devices simultaneously and may shut down unexpectedly. This can damage appliances due to voltage drops and leave you without critical power when you need it most, defeating the purpose of having backup power.
Can I use a larger generator than calculated?
Yes, a larger generator is generally safe and provides more flexibility. However, ensure you're not oversizing excessively, as larger units consume more fuel and cost more. A generator 10-20% larger than recommended is reasonable for future expansion.
How do I find the wattage for my appliances?
Check the nameplate on the device itself, look in the owner's manual, or search online for the model number. Most appliances clearly display their wattage requirements. If a device lists amperage instead, multiply amps by volts (typically 120 or 240) to calculate watts.
